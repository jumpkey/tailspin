# DFW–Lafayette: One Corridor, in Plain Sight

**A focused case study in airline schedule fragility — what the public record shows
about a single route, and why it points to something larger.**

Compiled 2026-06-21 from public U.S. DOT (BTS On-Time Performance) and NOAA weather
records, January 2024 – April 2026. Every figure is reproducible; see the
methodology note at the end. This report is the *single-corridor* companion to the
network-wide study in [BIGRUN-FINDINGS-GUIDE.md](../BIGRUN-FINDINGS-GUIDE.md).

---

## The question that started this

*Why is flying American between Dallas/Fort Worth and Lafayette so unreliable —
especially when the weather is bad?*

It began as a personal observation. The public data turns it into something
measurable, and the answer is clearer than expected: **the corridor's schedule is
acutely sensitive to weather, no operator on it holds up well, and the operator
flying it has changed underneath the traveler — it is now almost entirely PSA
Airlines, one of American's wholly-owned regional carriers.** The same fragility
signature shows up across American's national network, which is the reason this
small route is worth a leadership audience.

---

## 1. The lead finding: bad weather breaks this schedule

Looking at 2024–2025, the chance that a DFW–LFT flight is **delayed an hour or more**
rises steeply from good weather to bad — for *every* regional operator on the route:

| Operator (American Eagle) | Good weather | Bad weather | **Weather amplification** | Bad-weather cancel rate |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Envoy** | 5.6% | 23.5% | **4.2× — steepest** | 6.6% |
| **PSA** | 12.9% | 28.8% | 2.2× | 10.8% |
| **SkyWest** | 6.0% | 16.4% | 2.7× | 11.5% |

Two distinct, both-true facts — and they answer the original question precisely:

- **Envoy is the most weather-*sensitive* operator** on the corridor: its schedule
  degrades the most sharply when weather turns (a 4.2× jump). That is the
  weather-stress pattern the original question suspected.
- **PSA is the worst in *absolute* terms** under bad weather: roughly **4 in 10**
  PSA flights are cancelled or badly delayed when weather is adverse (28.8% delayed
  + 10.8% cancelled).

The fairness check is built in: the three operators differ markedly from one
another on the *same route*, so this is not "Lafayette is just hard" and not "all
regional flying is bad." It is specific to operators and conditions.

## 2. The operator keeps changing — and it's PSA now

DFW–LFT does not have one steady operator. It **rotates** among three of American's
regional carriers, month to month. Crucially, that rotation has resolved: through
the most recent data (2026), the corridor is **overwhelmingly PSA**.

| Month (2026) | PSA | Envoy | SkyWest |
|---|---|---|---|
| Jan | 238 | 10 | 0 |
| Feb | 174 | 32 | 0 |
| Mar | 208 | 14 | 0 |
| Apr | 232 | 0 | 48 |

Envoy — historically a major operator here — has **all but exited the corridor**.
So a traveler's *recent* bad experience on DFW–LFT is, in practice, a **PSA**
experience: the operator with the highest absolute disruption rate in bad weather.
The lived inconsistency ("it used to be okay, now it's not") is partly the schedule
and partly *who is flying it changing underneath you*.

## 3. Did 2026 make it better? No — season-matched, it held or worsened

To compare fairly, 2026 (January–April, partial year) is measured against the
**same months** of 2024–2025 — removing the winter-weather bias that would
otherwise inflate 2026.

- **The corridor got worse — mainly because of who flies it now.** Corridor-wide
  disruption rose 15.1% → 16.8% (season-matched). But **PSA on its own barely
  changed** (17.1% → 17.7%): the corridor worsened chiefly because it **shifted to
  PSA**, its least-reliable operator, from 42% to **89%** of flights. More of your
  flights are now on the worst performer — that, more than any single operator
  declining, is why the experience degraded.
- **Knock-on (late-inbound-aircraft) delays rose ~39%** corridor-wide, and **PSA's
  bad-weather cancellations roughly doubled** (≈11% → ≈24%). *Caveat: only ~51
  adverse-weather PSA flights in the 2026 window — directional, not precise.*
- **Network-wide, the pattern persisted** (see §4): PSA remained the worst-
  performing operator and worsened even season-matched; Envoy remained the best
  and stable.

The honest read: 2026 shows **no improvement** on this corridor, and the broader
deterioration in PSA's reliability is visible network-wide, not just here.

## 4. Why this one corridor matters: it is not unusual

DFW–LFT is a single example of a pattern that repeats across American's entire
9-hub network (6.15M flights, 2024–2025; full detail in the network study):

- **PSA-operated routes are ~1 in 8 flights but about half of the network's worst
  trouble spots** — a roughly **4× over-representation**. A second wholly-owned
  regional, Envoy, is *under*-represented — which is why this is an
  operating-structure finding, not an anti-regional one.
- **The PSA-operated DFW–LFT cell ranks #63 of 1,668** route-operator combinations
  — the **worst ~4%** of the network.
- **The trouble is structural, not about big airports.** American's largest hubs by
  traffic (LAX, Phoenix, JFK) are among its *cleanest*; the worst severe-delay hubs
  are Chicago-O'Hare and Washington-National — and **both worsened into 2026**.
- **It is dominated by knock-on delays** — one late aircraft cascading through the
  day — more than by weather itself.

So the corridor a single traveler experiences as chronically unreliable sits inside
a measurable, repeating, nationwide structure — all of it visible from outside the
airline using only public records.

## 5. What each leader needs to know — and could do

*Framed as observations from public data, not allegations. The recommended actions
are diagnostic, not disciplinary.*

**American Airlines — CEO / Network strategy**
- *Know:* A specific, repeatable fragility pattern is visible in American's branded
  product from public data alone; DFW–LFT is one of hundreds of instances, and the
  national pattern persisted into 2026.
- *Do:* Confirm whether this same systematic, network-wide stress-test is run
  internally — and if these routes already appear on it. If a passenger can find
  the worst 4% in an afternoon, the question is why it would not already be on the
  internal radar.

**American — Network Planning / Scheduling**
- *Know:* Fragility concentrates in thin regional spokes at DFW, ORD, and DCA and is
  dominated by knock-on delays — a routing/turn-time/bank-architecture signature,
  not pure weather; and the worst hubs worsened into 2026.
- *Do:* Examine equipment routing and turn buffers on the most-affected spokes;
  compare DFW/ORD/DCA structures against the clean high-volume hubs (LAX/PHX).

**American — Regional / American Eagle oversight**
- *Know:* Two wholly-owned regionals diverge sharply — PSA over-represented in the
  worst routes and worsening, Envoy consistently cleaner — flying the same brand
  and schedule. DFW–LFT shifted from Envoy to PSA and did not improve.
- *Do:* Run a direct PSA-vs-Envoy comparison on matched routes/weather to isolate
  what differs (assignments, fleet, crewing) from what is common (the schedule).

**PSA Airlines**
- *Know:* PSA-operated routes carry a disproportionate share of the network's worst
  reliability, with a bad-weather and knock-on-delay signature, and PSA's metrics
  worsened into 2026 even after controlling for season.
- *Do:* Review the most-affected spokes; the schedule PSA flies is set upstream, so
  the constructive step is a joint PSA–American look at where routing/recovery is
  breaking down.

**Envoy Air**
- *Know:* The data is, on balance, favorable to Envoy — under-represented in the
  worst routes, the steadiest of the regionals 2024→2026. Its one notable trait is
  high *weather sensitivity* on certain spokes (sharp good→bad degradation), even
  where its overall rate is low.
- *Do:* Treat the weather-sensitivity spikes as the targeted item; the broader
  reliability picture is a relative strength worth protecting.

## 6. The single argument for looking into this

> The entire analysis behind this report uses **only public U.S. government data**,
> cost nothing, and reproduces in about an hour. It identifies — by route, operator,
> weather, and year — exactly where American's branded schedule is most likely to
> fail a passenger, and shows the pattern holding into 2026. The compelling question
> is not "what is wrong with one route." It is: **if an outside observer can see this
> clearly from public records, is the same being seen and acted on inside — and if
> not, why not?**

## Methodology & honest limitations

- **Data:** BTS On-Time Performance (every scheduled U.S. domestic flight) joined to
  NOAA ASOS weather; 100% weather-match. "Bad weather" = the adverse bucket;
  "badly delayed" = arrival ≥ 60 minutes (DOT-standard).
- **2026 is partial** (Jan–Apr, the months BTS had published) and is always compared
  **season-matched** to the same months of 2024–2025.
- **Small samples are flagged, not hidden.** Weather-stratified 2026 counts on a
  single corridor are small (tens of flights); those figures are directional.
- **Association, not causation.** Outcomes reflect schedule design, fleet/crew
  routing, weather, and ATC together. PSA and Envoy are wholly-owned American
  subsidiaries flying American's schedule and brand; the structural questions are
  American's.
- **Reproducible.** Source code and data manifests are public at [repository URL].
