This is the *strategic / governance* letter, pitched higher than the

[Date]

[CEO Name]
Chief Executive Officer, American Airlines Group

Dear [Name],

I am a frequent American customer. I am also someone who works with data, and I
recently spent about an hour doing something I think should give you pause: using
only public records, I mapped schedule reliability across American's entire hub
network and found where cancellations and cascading delays concentrate — down to
specific routes and operators.

I am writing to you, rather than only to your operating teams, because the most
important finding is not any single route. **It is that this was possible at all,
from the outside, with free public data.**

**What I'd want to know if I sat in your chair:**

- Across American's nine hubs and 24 months (6.15 million flights), schedule
  fragility is **concentrated and stable** — a small set of route-operator
  combinations carries a disproportionate share of disruption, and it stays put
  no matter how the analysis is sliced.
- It tracks **operating structure, not weather or volume.** Your largest hubs by
  traffic (LAX, Phoenix, JFK) are among your *cleanest*. The trouble sits in thin
  regional spokes feeding DFW, Chicago, and Washington-National, and it is
  dominated by *knock-on* delays — one late aircraft cascading through the day.
- It is **specific, not a blanket regional complaint.** One of your wholly-owned
  regional carriers shows materially *better* reliability than the other on the
  very same kinds of routes. I went out of my way to confirm this, because it is
  the difference between a fair finding and an unfair one.
- Everything is built from the **U.S. DOT's own public data** and is fully
  reproducible. I will hand you the method and the code (below).

**The one question I'd ask of your organization:**

> Does American run this same analysis internally — systematically, across the
> whole network — and if so, do these routes already appear on it? If a customer
> with a laptop can find your worst-performing 4% in an afternoon, your teams can
> surely do better. The question is whether they are, and what happens when they
> do.

I'll make this concrete with one corridor I know personally: DFW–Lafayette. When
the weather turns, its schedule comes apart — and depending on which of three
rotating regional carriers happens to be flying it that month, a passenger's odds
of a cancellation or a long delay swing widely. That is one small corridor. The
network pattern says it is not unusual.

**What I am asking you to do:** simply to look — to have someone confirm whether
this is already on your radar and, if not, why a public-data analysis surfaced it
first. I have no commercial interest and I am not seeking anything from American
beyond better-run flights on the routes I and many others depend on.

To remove any doubt about the analysis, I am making the **complete source code and
methodology publicly available** at [repository URL], so your teams — or anyone —
can reproduce every figure from the public data. I would genuinely welcome being
told where, if anywhere, I have it wrong.

Respectfully,

[Your name]
